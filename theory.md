Tortilla chips are a widely consumed brittle food susceptible to mechanical damage during packaging, transport, and handling. Fracture reduces product quality and consumer satisfaction, leading to economic losses for manufacturers. Understanding the properties that dictate fracture behaviour is practical for food engineering and packaging design on an industrial scale.

Water plays an important role in determining the mechanical behaviour of starchy foods by increasing their flexibility and softness, a trait called "plasticization" in food engineering (__Braga and Cunha, 2002__). As moisture content increases, water molecules disrupt intermolecular hydrogen bonds within the starch-gluten matrix, decreasing the elastic modulus, E, and increasing ductility. Below a critical moisture threshold, starchy materials undergo a glass transition, shifting from a rubbery, compliant state to a glassy, brittle one, which substantially alters fracture behaviour (__Roos, 1995__).

Fracture energy, G, quantifies the energy per unit area required to propagate a crack through a material and is a more physically meaningful descriptor of brittle failure than peak force alone, as it integrates both the magnitude and displacement of the fracture event (__Atkins and Mai, 1985__). For a thin disc under central punch loading, G is related to the strain energy to first fracture U and fracture surface area A by:

$$G_c = \frac{U}{A}$$

where U is obtained by integrating the force-displacement curve to the first peak:

$$U = \int_0^{\delta_c} F(\delta) \, d\delta$$

and A is approximated as the chip cross-sectional area given the standardised commercial geometry. Several complementary formulations exist for characterising brittle fracture in disc geometries. From thin plate theory, the critical stress at the bottom surface of a centrally loaded disc on a ring support is:

$$\sigma_c = \frac{3F}{4\pi t^2} \left[ (1 + \nu) \ln\left(\frac{R}{r}\right) + (1 - \nu)\frac{R^2 - r^2}{2R^2} \right]$$

where F is the applied force at fracture, t is chip thickness, R is the support ring radius, r is the punch radius, and v is Poisson's ratio, typically assumed as 0.3 for starchy brittle materials (__Timoshenko and Woinowsky-Krieger, 1959__). From linear elastic fracture mechanics, the critical stress intensity factor K_Ic relates to fracture energy through:

$$K_{Ic} = \sqrt{G_c E}$$

where E is the elastic modulus, recoverable from the pre-fracture linear slope of the force-displacement curve via:

$$E = \frac{F}{w} \cdot \frac{3(1-\nu^2)R^2}{4t^3}$$

with w being the central deflection at load F (__Timoshenko and Woinowsky-Krieger, 1959__). Together these formulations allow fracture behaviour to be characterised from multiple angles using a single force-displacement trace, connecting measurable quantities F, t, R, and w to the underlying material properties G_c, K_Ic, and E. This study investigates the relationship between moisture content and fracture energy in commercially produced tortilla chips, using a custom biaxial punch-on-ring apparatus across a range of controlled bake times.
